Bird Species

Australasian Bittern

Australasian Bitterns specialise in living in dense beds of reeds and rushes, where they are surprisingly difficult to see, as they are particularly well camouflaged among reeds. Added to this, when alarmed, they stand still with neck stretched upwards and bill pointing skywards.
Australasian Darter

Because of its long and slender neck, the Australasian Darter is sometimes called the snakebird. Usually inhabiting freshwater wetlands, darters swim with their bodies submerged beneath the water’s surface, with only the sinuous neck protruding above the water, enhancing its serpentine qualities.
Australasian Grebe

The Australasian Grebe is usually confined to freshwater wetlands, and can often be seen swimming singly or in twos on farm dams. They build floating nests — a platform made from green aquatic vegetation — into which bluish-white eggs are laid, sometimes by two females.
Australasian Pipit

When seen in grassland habitats, Australasian Pipits could easily be mistaken for introduced Eurasian Skylarks, but make pipits are a native species. Australasian Pipits were once considered to be the Australasian representative of the widespread Richard’s Pipit.
Australasian Shoveler

Often associating with other species of ducks, the Australasian Shoveler is often seen in flocks with Pink-eared Ducks. They inhabit a wide variety of wetlands, ranging from terrestrial swamps and lakes to estuaries and even sheltered inshore waters.
Australian Hobby

One of the alternative names for the Australian Australian is the ‘Little Duck Hawk’, which is more than a little misleading. Although Hobbies regularly eat birds, they are not able to kill birds that are bigger than they are, and Hobbies are smaller than most ducks.
Australian Reed-Warbler

In southern Australian wetlands, one of the sounds of summer is the melodious song of the Australian Reed-Warbler. This species is migratory in south-eastern parts of the country, arriving in spring and then moving north and north-west in late summer and early autumn. The species’ movements in northern Australia and the South West are less-clearly understood.
Australian Pelican

The Australian Pelican is often seen around the coasts, where it can be seen roosting on sandbanks, rock platforms and reefs, or swimming in lagoons, bays and estuarine waters, dipping their oversized bills into the water to catch fish.
Australian Shelduck

With a rich-chestnut breast contrasting with a white ring-neck and sooty-black head and body, the Australian Shelduck is a handsome waterfowl. Shelducks sometimes congregate in flocks of more than a thousand birds, especially when moulting, and usually occur at large or deep wetlands, or in grasslands, pasture or crops.
Australian White Ibis

In regional areas, Australian White Ibis (and Straw-necked Ibis) are sometimes called ‘the farmers’ friend’, due to their habit of flocking into areas afflicted by plagues of locusts and gorging on the ravaging hoards of insects.
